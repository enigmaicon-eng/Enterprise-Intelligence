## Definition
Mental models are simplified representations of how systems work — frameworks for making decisions faster and more reliably by pattern-matching to proven structure rather than reasoning from scratch each time. In strategic work, a small number of high-quality mental models applied consistently outperforms a large number of models applied inconsistently.

## Why It Matters
Without explicit mental models, decisions default to availability bias (what's most recent), sunk cost reasoning, and narrative coherence over evidence. Mental models don't eliminate bias but make it visible. Making the model explicit allows the model to be challenged.

## Key Mental Models in Active Use

### Inversion
Instead of asking "how do I succeed?", ask "how do I fail — and what does avoiding that look like?" Useful for pre-mortems, risk identification, and argument stress-testing. Applied via the `architecture/ANTI-PATTERNS.md` framework and the decision journal's pre-mortem section.

### Second-Order Thinking
Every decision has first-order effects (obvious, intended) and second-order effects (downstream consequences). Weak strategists optimize for first-order. Strong strategists ask "and then what?" at least twice. Applied before any high-reversibility decision.

### Regret Minimization
When facing an uncertain bet: imagine yourself at 80 reviewing the decision. Which choice would you regret not making more — acting or not acting? Particularly useful for "good enough vs. wait for more data" traps. Cuts through analysis paralysis.

### Map vs. Territory
The model, plan, or framework is not the reality. When observed behavior diverges from the plan, the territory (observation) wins, not the map (plan). Applied when data contradicts narrative: update the map.

### Asymmetric Bets
Seek decisions where downside is bounded and upside is large. Avoid decisions where upside is bounded and downside is unbounded. The bet portfolio in `strategy/active-bets.md` scores bets on this axis explicitly.

### Reversibility Filter
Before committing: is this a one-way door (irreversible, costly to undo) or two-way door (reversible, low cost to change)? Two-way doors should be decided fast and cheaply. One-way doors deserve the full decision protocol. Mis-classifying a one-way door as two-way is the most common expensive mistake.

## Failure Modes
- **Model stacking without application**: knowing 20 mental models but applying none consistently. One model applied every time beats twenty applied sometimes.
- **Retroactive model selection**: choosing the model that justifies the decision you've already made emotionally. Models must constrain decisions, not explain them post-hoc.
- **Context mismatch**: applying inversion to a high-uncertainty creative decision produces paralysis, not clarity. Match model to context.
