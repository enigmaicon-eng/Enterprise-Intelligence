## The Fundamental PM Constraint

PMs own outcomes but control almost nothing. They don't control engineering capacity, design resources, sales priorities, executive attention, or legal timelines. They influence all of these through positioning, relationships, and information architecture.

This is not a problem to solve — it is the nature of the role. The PM who spends energy trying to control things they don't control will be exhausted and ineffective. The PM who masters influence will be effective and often underestimated.

## Reading the Power Landscape

Before any major initiative, map the power landscape:

**Who cares?** List every person and group with a stake in the outcome — not just the obvious ones. Include: engineering leadership, sales, legal, finance, customers, support, data teams, partner teams, executives.

**What do they need to feel?** Not just "what do they need to know." Stakeholders make decisions from a mix of logic and identity. Understanding what they need to feel — safe, consulted, heard, credited, protected — is as important as understanding what they need to know.

**What's their current model?** What do they believe about this problem or initiative right now? Don't assume neutrality — most stakeholders have a prior position, often based on incomplete or outdated information.

**What's their BATNA?** What will they do if this initiative doesn't get their support? Understanding the alternative helps you frame your proposal relative to the alternative they'll take.

**Who influences whom?** The org chart is not the influence map. Find the relationships that actually move decisions — the engineer whose technical credibility shapes executive views, the sales rep whose customer relationships define what's possible, the chief of staff who controls calendar access.

## Stakeholder Patterns

**The blocker:** Appears in every planning process with reasons why something can't happen. Engage directly and specifically: "What would need to be true for you to support this?" The answer is usually either a solvable concern or a fundamental disagreement worth surfacing early.

**The skeptic:** Doubts the strategy or execution but expresses it indirectly — through questions, delays, omitting support, or going quiet. Skeptics are valuable — they see real risks. Create explicit spaces for their concerns rather than dismissing them as politics.

**The advocate:** Believes in the initiative and will carry it in rooms where you're not present. Invest in advocates: give them the clearest possible articulation of the strategy, the strongest talking points, and early access to wins.

**The politically motivated:** Supports or blocks based on what's good for their team or career, not the product. Navigate by finding the overlap between their interest and the initiative's success. Often you can create that overlap through credit-sharing, resourcing commitments, or influence over scope.

**The absentee:** Nominally important but disengaged. Don't wait for them to engage — go to them with a specific ask and a specific decision. Ambiguity breeds absence.

## The Alignment Architecture

Alignment is not a meeting — it is a system for progressively building shared understanding. Design it intentionally.

**Pre-alignment:** Before any formal review, have 1:1 conversations with the people whose support matters most. Not to lobby — to understand their perspective and surface concerns before they become meeting blockers. This is the single highest-leverage activity in any major initiative.

**The one-page:** A shared artifact that captures the strategy in its simplest form. Not for communication — for alignment testing. When different stakeholders read the same one-page and draw different conclusions, you've found the misalignment before it costs you.

**The DACI model:** For every major decision, make explicit: Driver (proposes and executes), Approver (can veto), Consulted (must be in the loop), Informed (need to know after). When DACI is unclear, every stakeholder fills the vacuum with their own decision rights assumptions.

**Alignment decay:** Alignment achieved at the start of a quarter decays by the end. Run periodic re-alignment: "Our strategy was X. Here's what we've learned. Here's what's changed. Here's what we're keeping. Is everyone still with us?" This prevents the late-quarter surprise where a stakeholder says they never agreed.

## Influence Without Authority: The Mechanics

**Information positioning:** The person who controls what information goes to whom in what framing controls the narrative. PMs who write crisp updates, route insights to the right people, and frame status in terms executives care about build disproportionate influence over time.

**Making their job easier:** Influence accumulates with every interaction where you make a peer's or leader's job easier. Come to legal with a proposed solution, not just a question. Come to engineering with a clear problem and explicit constraints, not a wishlist. Come to finance with modeled scenarios, not a budget ask.

**The credibility bank:** Every commitment you keep, every prediction that comes true, every result you deliver adds to your credibility account. Every missed commitment, oversold projection, or surprise withdrawal. PMs who make modest commitments and beat them consistently earn more latitude than those who promise big and deliver approximately.

**Framing the trade-off:** The most powerful influence move is framing a decision so the trade-off is clear and the PM's preferred option is obviously right given the organization's stated priorities. Not manipulation — clarity. "If we do A, we get X but give up Y. If we do B, we get Y but give up X. Given our strategy of [Z], B is the right call." When the logic is sound and the priorities are shared, this is not hard to say.

## Governance Thinking

Governance is the system for who makes which decisions and on what basis. Most product orgs have implicit governance — which means every decision is renegotiated from scratch.

**Decision rights mapping:** For each major decision type (roadmap commitments, pricing changes, architectural changes, feature kills, scope expansions), document: Who decides? Who must be consulted? What criteria govern the decision? When should it escalate?

**Escalation is not failure:** The PM who escalates a genuine disagreement clearly and quickly is doing their job. The PM who tries to resolve every disagreement at their level delays discovery of real misalignment. Escalate when: you've reached genuine impasse, the decision exceeds your authority, or the risk profile has changed significantly.

**Governance debt:** When governance is unclear or ignored, teams develop workarounds. Eventually the workarounds become de facto rules. When a crisis hits, the governance vacuum makes the crisis worse. Run periodic governance audits: are decision rights clear? Are escalation paths understood? Are the right people in the right decisions?

## Executive Communication as a Governance Mechanism

Executives don't manage products — they manage uncertainty. Your job in executive communication is to reduce their uncertainty about:
1. Whether the strategy is right
2. Whether the team is executing it well
3. Whether there are risks they need to know about
4. What decision, if any, is needed from them

Format for executive communication: Situation → Complication → Resolution. Not status reports — decision framing. See `prompts/pm/executive-brief.md` for the executable prompt.
