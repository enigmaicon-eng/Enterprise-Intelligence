## Definition

Product strategy is a coherent theory of how your product creates, captures, and sustains value — stated precisely enough to make prioritization obvious and disagreement productive.

Not: a list of features, themes, or OKRs.
Not: a mission statement.
Not: a roadmap.

Strategy is the causal chain between your current position and your target position, and the bets that bridge them.

## Why It Matters

Without explicit strategy, prioritization defaults to whoever has the most political capital. Discovery questions become free-floating. Stakeholders fill the vacuum with their own narratives. The product becomes reactive — a collection of reasonable decisions that add up to no position.

With explicit strategy, every prioritization decision has a reference point: does this compound our position or not? Every discovery question has a frame: what assumption does this test? Every executive conversation starts from alignment, not from scratch.

## The Strategy Articulation Standard

A product strategy is complete when it answers these five questions precisely:

**1. What game are we playing?**
The market, the user population, and the problem scope. Not broad ("enterprise software") but specific enough to make tradeoffs. "Helping mid-market logistics companies reduce carrier management overhead" is a game. "Improving supply chain" is not.

**2. Why do we believe we can win it?**
The theory of advantage. This is usually one of: network effects, data flywheel, switching costs, brand/trust, speed/timing, distribution access, or unique capability. Most products have one primary advantage source and one or two secondary ones. Be honest about which one.

**3. What must we believe for that to be true?**
The load-bearing assumptions. List the 3-5 assumptions whose failure would invalidate the strategy. These are not risks to manage — they are hypotheses to test. If you can't list them, you don't understand your strategy.

**4. What are we NOT doing?**
Strategy without explicit exclusion is not strategy. Name the adjacent opportunities, user segments, or capabilities you are explicitly not pursuing. This is where most strategy documents fail — the "what we're not doing" section reveals whether the strategy is real.

**5. How do we know if we're winning?**
The leading indicators of strategic progress, not just quarterly metrics. If network effects are your advantage, the leading indicator is engagement density, not revenue. If switching costs, it's depth of integration, not retention rate.

## Three Horizon Discipline

Horizon 1 (0-6 months): Execution confidence. Things you'd bet serious money on delivering. These should generate cash/retention/growth now.

Horizon 2 (6-18 months): Validation bets. Things you believe but haven't proven. Each has an explicit hypothesis and a kill condition.

Horizon 3 (18+ months): Option value. Things you're creating conditions for, not committing to. Premature commitment to H3 destroys H1/H2.

**Common failure:** Treating H2 items as H1 (over-commits, misses current customers) or H3 items as H2 (burns resources on unvalidated bets).

**Operational use:** In planning cycles, every initiative should map to a horizon. H1 items don't need strategy review. H2 items need hypothesis and kill conditions documented. H3 items need only "we're watching this" and explicit triggers for promotion to H2.

## Platform Thinking: When Products Become Leverage

Platform thinking applies when: your product enables third parties to create value, or when your infrastructure has more leverage as a shared layer than as a product-specific feature.

The platform question is not "should we build a platform?" It is "at which layers should we be a platform, and at which layers should we be a product?"

**Platform vs. product decision matrix:**

Build as platform when:
- Multiple teams/customers need the same capability
- The capability is a commodity that should be cheap
- Third-party innovation would accelerate your ecosystem
- Control of the layer gives strategic leverage

Build as product when:
- Differentiation lives in the experience, not the infrastructure
- Speed and tight integration matter more than openness
- The capability is core to your advantage

**Platform trap:** Building a platform before you have product-market fit. Platforms multiply leverage — if your direction is wrong, they multiply the cost of the wrong direction.

## Portfolio Thinking for PMs

Even a single-product PM has a portfolio: features, experiments, technical investments, partnerships. Portfolio thinking means:

- Explicit coverage across time horizons (not all H1 or all H3)
- Explicit coverage across risk profiles (not all safe bets or all moonshots)
- Explicit bet sizing (not everything is a "priority")
- Explicit review cycles (when do we kill a bet vs. stay the course?)

**Portfolio health check:**
- What % of capacity is on H1 vs. H2 vs. H3? (Target: roughly 70/20/10 for mature products)
- What's the ratio of feature work to capability work to technical work? (Capacity fully absorbed by features signals debt accumulation)
- How many bets have explicit kill conditions? (If none, you're not running bets — you're running commitments)

## Strategy Stress Tests

Apply these before presenting a strategy:

**The competitor test:** If your top competitor copied your strategy exactly, would it work for them? If yes, your strategy has no differentiation. If no, you have found your actual advantage.

**The user test:** Can you describe the specific moment where a specific user chooses your product over the alternative, and what specifically causes that choice? If this requires more than two sentences, the strategy isn't crisp enough.

**The engineering test:** Does your engineering team, having read the strategy, know which technical investments to make without asking you? If not, the strategy doesn't have enough operational specificity.

**The kill test:** Under what circumstances would you abandon this strategy? If you can't answer this in 30 seconds, you have a religion, not a strategy.
