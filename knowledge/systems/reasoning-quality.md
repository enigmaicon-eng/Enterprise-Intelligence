# Reasoning Quality

## Definition

Reasoning quality is the degree to which a chain of inference produces conclusions that hold under adversarial scrutiny — not just conclusions that feel coherent. High-quality reasoning is transparent, evidence-grounded, assumption-explicit, and falsifiable.

## Why It Matters

Most reasoning failures aren't caused by missing information. They're caused by structural flaws: conclusions that don't follow from premises, analogies that don't transfer, assumptions that aren't stated. Diagnosing reasoning quality means checking the structure, not just the conclusion.

## Key Principles

**1. State assumptions before drawing conclusions**
Any claim rests on premises. Unstated premises are the most common source of disagreement that seems like a factual dispute but is actually a structural dispute. Surface the assumption; the disagreement resolves.

**2. Distinguish evidence from interpretation**
Evidence: what the data shows. Interpretation: what the data means. These are always separate steps. Conflating them produces overconfident conclusions and blocks productive critique.

**3. Inversion as diagnostic**
Before committing to a position: what would have to be true for the opposite to be right? If the answer is "nothing I can name," the position is unfalsifiable. Unfalsifiable claims aren't wrong — they're unloadable; they can't be updated with new evidence.

**4. Second-order thinking as quality gate**
First-order: if X, then Y. Second-order: if X, then Y, and then what? Second-order thinking surfaces the downstream consequences that change whether Y is desirable. Skip it and you optimize locally while degrading globally.

**5. Analogical validity requires explicit transfer criteria**
Analogies are shortcuts. They're valid only when the relevant structural features of the source domain actually hold in the target domain. State those features explicitly; test whether they hold; if they don't, the analogy breaks.

**6. Confidence calibration**
Confidence should track evidence quality, not emotional investment. Signs of miscalibration: certainty on questions where experts disagree, uncertainty on questions where the evidence is overwhelming, confidence that doesn't update after encountering new evidence.

## In Practice

**Constraint-based stress-test:**
Take a conclusion you hold confidently. Apply this constraint: defend it using only evidence from a single file or source. No analogies. No appeals to general principle. What remains? What had to go? What that had to go was borrowed confidence.

**Assumption mapping:**
Before finalizing any recommendation, list: (1) what must be true for this to work, (2) what must be false for this to fail, (3) which of these have you tested, (4) which are you taking on faith. Untested assumptions under conditions where you could test them are a reasoning failure, not just a gap.

**Falsifiability check:**
For every prediction embedded in a strategy or decision: what observation would tell you the prediction was wrong? If you can't name an observation, the prediction is decorative.

## Common Reasoning Failure Modes

| Failure | Description | Detection Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Premise hiding | Conclusion stated without surfacing required assumptions | "Obviously" or "clearly" in the argument |
| Evidence-interpretation conflation | Data and meaning presented as single step | "The data shows that we should..." |
| Analogy overapplication | Transfer of non-existent structural similarity | Cross-domain claim without stated basis |
| Confidence anchoring | Initial estimate resists update from new evidence | Position same after new evidence as before |
| Premature closure | Conclusion locked before alternatives evaluated | "The answer is X" early in analysis |
| Survivorship reasoning | Only successful cases used to draw pattern | "All the best teams do X" |
